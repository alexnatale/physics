Phys 217 Homework Assignments – Spring 2025 (Revised)

Homework 5: Static Equilibrium, Gravitation, and Oscillations

Due: Tuesday, June 10 2025, 11:59 PM

Unless stated otherwise, use g = 9.81 m s-2.

Problem 1 (Uniform beam on two supports): A uniform 3.0 m beam of mass 35 kg rests horizontally on two scale supports 2.40 m apart. A 24 kg tool chest sits 0.60 m from the left end.
(a) Identify all forces acting on the beam and state their directions (no diagram needed).
(b) Calculate the reading on the left-hand scale.
(c) Calculate the reading on the right-hand scale.
(d) If the tool chest is slid toward the right end, at what distance from the left end will the right-hand scale first read zero?

Problem 2 (Leaning ladder with frictionless wall): A 5.0 m, 18 kg ladder rests against a vertical, frictionless wall making a 65° angle with the floor. A 75 kg painter stands 3.5 m up the ladder measured along its length.
(a) List all forces acting on the ladder, including their points of application and directions.
(b) Find the magnitude of the static-friction force Ff at the floor.
(c) Determine the minimum coefficient of static friction μs required so the ladder does not slip.
(d) If μs = 0.44, how high can the painter safely climb before the ladder begins to slip?

Problem 3 (Stress, strain, and elastic limit – conceptual + numerical): A 2.0 m steel rod (Young’s modulus 2.0 × 1011 Pa) of cross-sectional area 1.5 cm2 supports a 1.2 × 104 N load.
(a) Explain the physical difference between stress and strain.
(b) Compute the tensile stress on the rod.
(c) Compute the resulting strain and the elongation ΔL.
(d) The proportional (elastic) limit of this steel is 250 MPa. Does the rod remain within its elastic range? Justify.

Problem 4 (Earth–Moon force comparison – conceptual only): Using Newton’s third law, compare the gravitational force that Earth exerts on the Moon with the force the Moon exerts on Earth. Then give one observable effect on Earth caused by this mutual interaction.

Problem 5 (Escape velocity and impact speed): An asteroid of mass 6.0 × 109 kg approaches Earth on a collision course. Far from Earth (effectively at infinity) its speed is 2.0 km s-1.
(a) State the conservation-of-energy equation you would use to find the asteroid’s speed at a distance r from Earth’s centre (no derivation required).
(b) Use that equation to compute its speed at the top of the atmosphere (r ≈ R⊕ + 100 km).
(c) If 12 % of the asteroid’s kinetic energy is converted into thermal radiation when it fragments, estimate the energy (J) deposited as heat.
(d) Qualitatively discuss how Earth’s gravitational binding energy would differ for a Mars-sized planet.

Problem 6 (Circular vs elliptical satellite orbits): A spacecraft orbits Earth in a 300 km circular orbit. Its mission then requires a transfer to an elliptical orbit whose apogee is 35 786 km (geosynchronous altitude).
(a) Calculate the period of the initial circular orbit.
(b) Using conservation of energy, find the speed at perigee (300 km altitude) for the new elliptical orbit.
(c) Show that the spacecraft’s total mechanical energy is negative and interpret the sign.
(d) Describe qualitatively how Kepler’s second law manifests during one revolution in the elliptical orbit.

Problem 7 (Exoplanet via Kepler’s third law – data synthesis): Astronomers detect a Jupiter-sized exoplanet orbiting a star of mass 1.4 M☉. The orbital period is 380 days.
(a) Use Kepler’s third law to compute the semimajor axis a of the planet’s orbit.
(b) Determine the orbital speed at periapsis if the orbit’s eccentricity is 0.22.
(c) Explain why measuring the star’s radial-velocity curve allows an independent check of part (a).
(d) Give one limitation of Kepler’s law when applied to close binary stars instead of star-planet systems.

Problem 8 (Simple harmonic motion – spring–mass): A 0.60 kg block on a frictionless horizontal surface is attached to an ideal spring (k = 45 N m-1) and oscillates with amplitude 0.12 m.
(a) Write x(t) if at t = 0 the block is at +0.12 m and moving toward equilibrium.
(b) Compute its period, frequency, and maximum speed.
(c) Determine the block’s kinetic and potential energies when x = +0.06 m.
(d) If a small 0.20 kg mass is gently placed on top at the instant x = 0, find the new period, assuming the two masses stick together.

Problem 9 (Physical pendulum): A thin, uniform rod of length 1.2 m and mass 2.5 kg is pivoted about one end and allowed to oscillate in a vertical plane.
(a) Write the standard formula for the small-angle period T of a physical pendulum and substitute the moment of inertia for a uniform rod about one end to express T numerically (no derivation).
(b) Calculate the period.
(c) Compare this period to that of a simple pendulum of length 1.2 m and explain the difference.
(d) If the rod is released from a 15° displacement, find its angular speed as it passes through the lowest point.

Problem 10 (Damped and driven oscillations – conceptual focus): A mass–spring system experiences a damping force −bv and is subject to a sinusoidal driving force of amplitude F0.
(a) Describe qualitatively how the steady-state amplitude varies with driving frequency, noting the resonance peak (no sketch needed).
(b) Explain why the resonance frequency differs slightly from the natural frequency in the presence of damping.
(c) In a clock’s balance-wheel oscillator, why is a high quality factor (Q) desirable?
(d) Give one example where low-Q (heavy damping) is intentionally engineered and explain the benefit.
