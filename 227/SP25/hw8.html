Homework 8: Electromagnetic Induction and Inductance

Problem 1 (Faraday’s and Lenz’s Law): A circular loop of radius R = 0.10 m sits in a uniform magnetic field that is directed into the page. The magnitude of the magnetic field increases at a constant rate of 0.20 T/s.
(a) State Faraday’s law in integral form, ∮E·dl = –dΦB/dt, specifying the orientation of the loop’s normal.
(b) Using Lenz’s law, indicate whether the induced current is clockwise or counterclockwise as viewed from above.
(c) Calculate the magnitude of the induced emf in the loop.
(d) If the loop has resistance 5.0 Ω, determine the induced current.

Problem 2 (Motional emf in a sliding rod): A conducting rod of length ℓ = 0.30 m slides without friction on two parallel rails separated by d = 0.25 m. The rails are connected at one end by a resistor R = 2.0 Ω, forming a closed loop. A uniform magnetic field of magnitude B = 0.50 T is directed into the plane. The rod moves to the right at constant velocity v = 5.0 m/s.
(a) Write an expression for the motional emf induced between the ends of the rod, ε = B·d·v, where d = 0.25 m is the rail separation.
(b) Calculate the numerical value of this emf.
(c) Determine the current induced in the circuit.
(d) Compute the magnetic force on the rod and state its direction.

Problem 3 (Induced electric fields): Assume the solenoid has radius > 0.15 m so that the circular loop of radius r = 0.15 m lies entirely within its windings.
A long solenoid with n = 500 turns/m carries a current that decreases uniformly from 4.0 A to 0 A in 0.10 s. A circular loop of radius r = 0.15 m is coaxial with the solenoid.
(a) Calculate the initial magnetic flux through the loop.
(b) Use Faraday’s law to find the induced emf in the loop.
(c) Determine the magnitude of the induced electric field at distance r from the axis.

Problem 4 (Eddy currents and energy dissipation): A thick, nonmagnetic metal plate falls with constant speed through a region of uniform magnetic field. The induced eddy currents dissipate mechanical energy as heat.
(a) Explain qualitatively why the plate reaches a terminal velocity.
(b) Identify the physical principle that relates lost mechanical energy to induced currents.
(c) Discuss how changing the plate’s electrical conductivity affects its terminal speed.

Problem 5 (Back emf in electric generators): A single-loop generator has N = 100 turns, loop area A = 0.020 m², and rotates at angular speed ω = 50 rad/s in a uniform magnetic field B = 0.10 T. The loop circuit has total resistance R = 2.0 Ω.
(a) Derive an expression for the amplitude of the induced emf.
(b) Calculate its numerical value.
(c) When connected to a resistive load drawing current I, a back emf opposes rotation. Determine the steady-state current.
(d) Explain qualitatively how increasing ω affects both back emf and load current.

Problem 6 (Mutual inductance in coupled coils): Two tightly coupled coils have mutual inductance M = 0.020 H. Coil 1’s current changes at a rate dI₁/dt = 10 A/s. Coil 2 has resistance R₂ = 5.0 Ω.
(a) Calculate the magnitude of the induced emf in coil 2.
(b) Determine the induced current in coil 2 and its direction relative to the change in I₁.
(c) Explain how the induced emf would change if the coils were partially separated.

Problem 7 (RL circuit time response): A series RL circuit has R = 10 Ω and L = 2.0 H connected to a DC source V = 20 V.
(a) Write the differential equation for the current I(t) after the switch closes at t = 0, assuming I(0) = 0.
(b) Solve for I(t) and identify the time constant τ.
(c) Calculate how long it takes for I(t) to reach 90% of its final value.

Problem 8 (Energy stored in magnetic fields): An inductor of L = 0.50 H carries a current I = 3.0 A.
(a) Calculate the energy stored in the inductor.
(b) If this energy is released uniformly over 0.10 s, what power is delivered?
(c) For a solenoid inductor of length ℓ = 0.50 m, cross-sectional area A = 1.0×10⁻³ m², and turn density n = 1000 turns/m carrying I = 3.0 A, compute the energy density in the magnetic field.

Problem 9 (Rotating coil generator): Assume the rectangular coil of width w = 0.10 m and height h = 0.20 m rotates about its left vertical edge at angular speed ω = 100 rad/s in a uniform magnetic field B directed perpendicular to the coil’s plane when θ = 0.
(a) Write an expression for the instantaneous emf ε(t) in terms of θ(t) = ωt.
(b) Calculate the amplitude of ε(t).
(c) If the coil is connected to R = 5.0 Ω, determine the maximum current.
(d) Describe qualitatively how back emf alters the mechanical torque required.

Problem 10 (Real-world application – induction cooktop): Induction cooktops heat cookware via eddy currents generated by changing magnetic fields.
(a) Explain how eddy currents produce heat in the cookware.
(b) Discuss why the cookware must be ferromagnetic for efficient heating.
(c) Identify one safety feature that protects users from stray fields.
