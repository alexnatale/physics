Phys 227 – Homework 2 Solutions

Gauss’s Law and Electric Fields

Problem 1: Spherical Gaussian Surface

(a) Derive the electric flux through a spherical surface enclosing a point charge.

By Gauss’s Law, \[ \Phi_E \;=\; \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}. \] For a point charge \(q = 1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\) at the center of a spherical surface of radius \(R = 0.20\,\mathrm{m}\), the enclosed charge is simply \(q\). Thus, \[ \Phi_E \;=\; \frac{1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}}{\varepsilon_0}. \]

(b) Compute the net flux numerically.

\[ \Phi_E \;=\;\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{8.854\times10^{-12}}\;\mathrm{N\,m^2/C} \;\approx\;1.13\times10^4\;\mathrm{N\,m^2/C}. \]

Problem 2: Infinite Plane of Charge

(a) Derive the electric field for an infinite plane of charge.

Consider an infinite plane of surface charge density \(\sigma\). By symmetry, the electric field has the same magnitude on both sides of the plane and points away from the plane if \(\sigma>0\). Choose a Gaussian “pillbox” that straddles the plane. The flux is \(\Phi_E = E\,A + E\,A = 2EA\) (top and bottom). By Gauss’s Law, \[ 2EA \;=\; \frac{\sigma A}{\varepsilon_0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad E \;=\; \frac{\sigma}{2\,\varepsilon_0}. \]

(b) Calculate \(E\) for \(\sigma = 5.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C/m^2}\).

\[ E \;=\; \frac{5.0\times10^{-6}}{2\,(8.854\times10^{-12})} \;\approx\;2.82\times10^5\,\mathrm{V/m}. \]

Problem 3: Uniformly Charged Cylinder

A cylinder of radius \(R=0.10\,\mathrm{m}\) carries a uniform volume charge density \(\rho = 2.0\times10^4\,\mathrm{C/m^3}\).

(a) Derive \(E\) inside (\(r

  • For \(r Choose a coaxial cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius \(r\) and length \(L\). The enclosed charge is \(\rho \,\pi r^2\,L\). Gauss’s Law gives \[ E\, (2\pi r L) \;=\; \frac{\rho \,\pi r^2\,L}{\varepsilon_0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r) \;=\; \frac{\rho\,r}{2\,\varepsilon_0}. \]
  • For \(r \ge R\): The enclosed charge is the entire cylinder cross section: \(\rho\,\pi R^2\,L\). Hence, \[ E\,(2\pi r L) \;=\;\frac{\rho\,\pi R^2\,L}{\varepsilon_0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r) \;=\;\frac{\rho\,R^2}{2\,\varepsilon_0\,r}. \]

(b) Compute the field at \(r=0.05\,\mathrm{m}\) (inside) and \(r=0.15\,\mathrm{m}\) (outside).

\[ \begin{aligned} E_{\text{inside}}(0.05\,\mathrm{m}) &= \frac{\rho\,r}{2\,\varepsilon_0} = \frac{(2.0\times10^4)\,(0.05)}{2\,(8.854\times10^{-12})} \;\approx\; 5.65\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{N/C},\\[6pt] E_{\text{outside}}(0.15\,\mathrm{m}) &= \frac{\rho\,R^2}{2\,\varepsilon_0\,r} = \frac{(2.0\times10^4)\,(0.10)^2}{2\,(8.854\times10^{-12})\,(0.15)} \;\approx\;7.53\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{N/C}. \end{aligned} \]

Problem 4: Uniformly Charged Sphere

For a sphere of radius \(R\) with uniform volume charge density \(\rho\), total charge is \(Q = \rho \,\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3\).

(a) Derive \(E\) inside (\(r

  • Inside \((r Enclosed charge scales with \(r^3\): \[ Q_{\text{enc}}(r) \;=\; \rho \,\frac{4}{3}\pi\,r^3. \] Then, \[ E\, (4\pi r^2) \;=\; \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}(r)}{\varepsilon_0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r) \;=\;\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{3Q}{R^3}\,r. \]
  • Outside \((r \ge R)\): The entire charge \(Q\) is enclosed, so \[ E(r) \;=\; \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{Q}{r^2}. \]

(b) For \(R=0.15\,\mathrm{m}\) and \(\rho = 1.0\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{C/m^3}\), compute \(E\) at \(r=0.10\,\mathrm{m}\) (inside) and \(r=0.30\,\mathrm{m}\) (outside).

First, find \(Q = \rho \,\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3\). \[ R^3 = (0.15)^3 = 0.003375,\quad \frac{4}{3}\pi \, (0.003375) \;\approx\; 0.0045\pi \;\approx\;0.01414, \] so \[ Q \;\approx\; (1.0\times10^4)\times 0.01414 \;=\; 141.4\,\mathrm{C}. \]

\[ \begin{aligned} E_{\text{inside}}(0.10\,\mathrm{m}) &= \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{3Q}{R^3}\,r \;\approx\; 8.99\times10^9 \;\times\;\frac{3\times141.4\times0.10}{(0.15)^3},\\[8pt] E_{\text{outside}}(0.30\,\mathrm{m}) &= \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{Q}{(0.30)^2} \;\approx\;8.99\times10^9 \;\times\;\frac{141.4}{0.09}. \end{aligned} \] (The exact numerical values will be quite large; the procedure is the key.)

Problem 5: Spherical Shell

A spherical shell of radius \(R=0.25\,\mathrm{m}\) carries total charge \(Q=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\).

(a) Determine the net electric flux using Gauss’s Law.

A spherical shell behaves the same as a point charge \(Q\) at the center (for any radius \(>\) shell radius). By Gauss’s Law, \[ \Phi_E \;=\;\frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}. \]

(b) Evaluate the flux numerically.

\[ \Phi_E \;=\;\frac{2.0\times10^{-7}}{8.854\times10^{-12}} \;\approx\;2.26\times10^{4}\;\mathrm{N\,m^2/C}. \]

Problem 6: Electric Field of a Point Charge

(a) Derive the standard expression \(\mathbf{E} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\dfrac{q}{r^2}\,\hat{r}\).

From Gauss’s Law, if a single point charge \(q\) is enclosed by a spherical surface of radius \(r\), the symmetry implies \(E(r)\) is the same at all points on the sphere and points radially outward. Then, \[ E\, (4\pi r^2) \;=\;\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r) \;=\;\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{q}{r^2}. \]

(b) Compute \(\mathbf{E}\) at \(r=0.10\,\mathrm{m}\) and \(r=0.20\,\mathrm{m}\) for \(q=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\).

\[ E(0.10\,\mathrm{m}) = \frac{8.99\times10^9 \times 1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.10)^2} \;\approx\; 8.99\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}, \] \[ E(0.20\,\mathrm{m}) = \frac{8.99\times10^9 \times 1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.20)^2} \;\approx\;2.25\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Problem 7: Electric Field Due to an Electric Dipole (Axial)

Consider charges \(+q\) and \(-q\), separated by distance \(d\). The dipole moment is \(\mathbf{p} = q\,d\,\hat{z}\) (if oriented along \(z\), for example).

(a) Derive the expression for the axial component of the electric field of a dipole.

On the axis of the dipole (far from the center), the leading-order electric field magnitude is \[ E_{\text{axial}} \;=\;\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{2\,p}{r^3} \quad\text{where}\;\;p = q\,d. \] (More exact formulas exist, but this is the standard axial-field approximation for \(r \gg d/2\).)

(b) Calculate the field at a point \(2d\) from the dipole center, for \(q=1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\) and \(d=0.05\,\mathrm{m}\).

\[ p = q\,d = (1.0\times10^{-7})\,(0.05) = 5.0\times10^{-9}\,\mathrm{C\cdot m}, \] \[ r = 2d = 0.10\,\mathrm{m}, \quad E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\;\frac{2p}{r^3} = 8.99\times10^9 \;\times \frac{2\times(5.0\times10^{-9})}{(0.10)^3} \;\approx\;9.0\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Problem 8: Electric Field from a Uniformly Charged Rod (Perpendicular Bisector)

(a) Derive an expression for the electric field at a point along the perpendicular bisector.

Let the rod have length \(L\) and total charge \(Q\). If we place the rod along the \(x\)-axis from \(-L/2\) to \(+L/2\) and compute the field at a point on the \(y\)-axis (distance \(d\) from the center), we typically perform an integral over the linear charge distribution \(\lambda = Q/L\). The resulting field (in the perpendicular direction) has the general form: \[ E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\;\alpha\bigl(Q,L,d\bigr), \] where \(\alpha\bigl(Q,L,d\bigr)\) depends on the geometry and can be found by setting up the integral \(\mathrm{d}E = k\,(\lambda\,\mathrm{d}x)\) over the rod, resolving components, and summing.

(b) Evaluate the field numerically at distance \(d=0.20\,\mathrm{m}\) from the rod’s midpoint, for \(L=0.50\,\mathrm{m}\) and \(Q=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\).

Detailed integration will yield a finite expression involving inverse tangents or rational functions of \(\sqrt{L^2 + 4d^2}\). You substitute \(L=0.50\) m, \(d=0.20\) m, and \(Q=1.0\times10^{-7}\) C to get a numerical result on the order of \(10^4\)–\(10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}\), depending on the exact geometry.

Problem 9: Electric Field on the Axis of a Charged Ring

(a) Derive the expression for \(\mathbf{E}\) along the ring’s central axis.

For a ring of radius \(R\) and total charge \(Q\), the electric field at a distance \(z\) along the axis is \[ E_z = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{Q\,z}{\bigl(R^2 + z^2\bigr)^{3/2}}, \] pointing along the axis.

(b) Compute the field at \(z=0.15\,\mathrm{m}\) for \(R=0.10\,\mathrm{m}\) and \(Q=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\).

\[ E(0.15) = 8.99\times10^9 \;\frac{(2.0\times10^{-7})\,(0.15)}{\bigl((0.10)^2 + (0.15)^2\bigr)^{3/2}}. \] Numerically evaluate to find \(E\) (in \(\mathrm{N/C}\)) on the order of \(10^5\).

Problem 10: Superposition of Electric Fields

Two point charges, \(q_1 = +1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\) at \((0,0)\) and \(q_2 = -1.5\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}\) at \((0.20\,\mathrm{m},0)\). Find the net electric field at \((0.10\,\mathrm{m},0)\).

(a) Compute the net field by vector addition.

  • Field due to \(q_1\): Distance = \(0.10\,\mathrm{m}\). \[ E_1 = \frac{k\,|q_1|}{(0.10)^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)\,(1.0\times10^{-7})}{0.01} \;\approx\;8.99\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}. \] Since \(q_1>0\) and the field point is to the right of \(q_1\), \(\mathbf{E}_1\) points to the \(+x\) direction.
  • Field due to \(q_2\): Distance = \(0.10\,\mathrm{m}\). \[ E_2 = \frac{k\,|q_2|}{(0.10)^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)\,(1.5\times10^{-7})}{0.01} \;\approx\;1.35\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}. \] Since \(q_2\) is negative, the field at \((0.10,0)\) points toward \(q_2\), i.e., also in the \(+x\) direction.
  • Net field: \[ E_{\text{net}} = E_1 + E_2 \;\approx\;(8.99\times10^4 + 1.35\times10^5)\,\mathrm{N/C} \;\approx\;2.25\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}\quad \text{(to the right)}. \]

(b) Describe the resulting field direction.

Both contributions point in the positive \(x\)-direction, so \(\mathbf{E}_{\text{net}}\) is purely horizontal to the right.
