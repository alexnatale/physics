Homework 2 Solutions: Gauss’s Law and Electric Fields

Solution to Problem 1 (Spherical Gaussian Surface):

Part (a):
Use Gauss’s Law to derive the electric flux \(\Phi_E\) through a spherical surface enclosing a point charge \(q = +1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) of radius \(R = 0.20\,m\).
By Gauss’s Law, \[ \Phi_E \;=\; \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}. \] Since the point charge \(q\) is enclosed, \(Q_{\text{enc}} = q\). Thus, \[ \Phi_E \;=\; \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}. \]

Part (b):
Numerically, \[ \Phi_E \;=\;\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{C}}{8.854\times10^{-12}\,\mathrm{C^2/(N\,m^2)}} \;\approx\;1.13\times10^4\;\mathrm{N\,m^2/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 2 (Infinite Plane of Charge):

Part (a):
For an infinite plane with surface charge density \(\sigma\), apply Gauss’s Law with a “pillbox” Gaussian surface: \[ 2EA \;=\;\frac{\sigma A}{\varepsilon_0} \;\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;\; E = \frac{\sigma}{2\,\varepsilon_0}. \]

Part (b):
For \(\sigma=5.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C/m^2}\): \[ E = \frac{5.0\times10^{-6}}{2\,(8.854\times10^{-12})} \;\approx\;2.82\times10^5\,\mathrm{V/m}. \]

Solution to Problem 3 (Uniformly Charged Cylinder):

A long cylinder of radius \(R=0.10\,m\) has a uniform volume charge density \(\rho=2.0\times10^4\,\mathrm{C/m^3}\).

Part (a):
Inside \((r A coaxial cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius \(r\) encloses charge \(\rho\,\pi r^2 L\). By Gauss’s Law, \[ E(2\pi r L) \;=\; \frac{\rho\,\pi r^2 L}{\varepsilon_0} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; E(r) = \frac{\rho\,r}{2\,\varepsilon_0}. \] Outside \((r \ge R)\): The enclosed charge is \(\rho\,\pi R^2 L\). Thus, \[ E(2\pi r L) = \frac{\rho\,\pi R^2 L}{\varepsilon_0} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; E(r) = \frac{\rho\,R^2}{2\,\varepsilon_0\,r}. \]

Part (b):
At \(r=0.05\,m\) (inside): \[ E_{\text{inside}}(0.05)=\frac{\rho\,r}{2\,\varepsilon_0} = \frac{(2.0\times10^4)(0.05)}{2(8.854\times10^{-12})}\approx5.65\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{N/C}. \] At \(r=0.15\,m\) (outside): \[ E_{\text{outside}}(0.15)=\frac{\rho\,R^2}{2\,\varepsilon_0\,r} = \frac{(2.0\times10^4)(0.10)^2}{2(8.854\times10^{-12})(0.15)}\approx7.53\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 5 (Spherical Shell):

A spherical shell of radius \(R=0.25\,m\) and total charge \(Q=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\).

Part (a):
By Gauss’s Law, the net flux through any sphere enclosing the shell is \[ \Phi_E=\frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}. \]

Part (b):
Numerically, \[ \Phi_E = \frac{2.0\times10^{-7}}{8.854\times10^{-12}} \;\approx\;2.26\times10^4\,\mathrm{N\,m^2/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 6 (Electric Field of a Point Charge):

Part (a):
From Gauss’s Law for a point charge \(q\), enclosed by a sphere of radius \(r\): \[ E(4\pi r^2)=\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r)=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q}{r^2}. \]

Part (b):
For \(q=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\), \[ E(0.10\,\mathrm{m}) = 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.10)^2} \approx 8.99\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}, \] \[ E(0.20\,\mathrm{m}) = 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.20)^2} \approx 2.25\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 7 (Electric Field of an Electric Dipole):

Part (a):
On the axis of an ideal dipole (charges \(\pm q\), separated by \(d\)), for large distances \(r \gg d\), the field magnitude is: \[ E_{\text{axial}} \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{2p}{r^3}, \quad\text{where } p=q\,d. \]

Part (b):
For \(q=1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\), \(d=0.05\,m\), then \(p=5.0\times10^{-9}\,\mathrm{C\cdot m}\). At a point \(2d=0.10\,m\) from the center: \[ E = 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{2(5.0\times10^{-9})}{(0.10)^3} \approx 9.0\times10^7\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 8 (Electric Field from a Uniformly Charged Rod):

Part (a):
For a rod of length \(L\) and total charge \(Q\), placed on the x‑axis from \(-L/2\) to \(+L/2\), the field at a point on the perpendicular bisector (distance \(d\) from the midpoint) requires integrating contributions from each element of charge.

We need to set up a coordinate system:

  • The rod lies along the x-axis from \(x = -L/2\) to \(x = +L/2\)
  • The point P where we want to find the field is at coordinates \((0, d)\)
  • The rod carries a total charge \(Q\)

The linear charge density \(\lambda\) is:

\[ \lambda = \frac{Q}{L} \]

For a small element \(dx\) at position \(x\), the charge is \(dq = \lambda\,dx\).

The distance from this element to point P is:

\[ r = \sqrt{x^2 + d^2} \]

This element creates a field \(d\vec{E}\) at point P:

\[ d\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{dq}{r^2} \cdot \hat{r} \]

The field has components:

\[ dE_x = dE \cdot \frac{x}{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{\lambda \, dx}{r^2} \cdot \frac{x}{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{\lambda \, x \, dx}{r^3} \]

\[ dE_y = dE \cdot \frac{d}{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{\lambda \, dx}{r^2} \cdot \frac{d}{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{\lambda \, d \, dx}{r^3} \]

Due to symmetry, the \(x\)-components from elements at \(+x\) and \(-x\) cancel out. So we only need the \(y\)-component.

The total electric field in the \(y\)-direction is:

\[ E_y = \int_{-L/2}^{+L/2} dE_y = \int_{-L/2}^{+L/2} \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{\lambda \, d \, dx}{(x^2 + d^2)^{3/2}} \]

Factoring out constants:

\[ E_y = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \lambda \, d \cdot \int_{-L/2}^{+L/2} \frac{dx}{(x^2 + d^2)^{3/2}} \]

To solve this integral, use the substitution \(u = x/d\):

  • \(dx = d \, du\)
  • When \(x = -L/2\), \(u = -L/(2d)\)
  • When \(x = L/2\), \(u = L/(2d)\)

The integral becomes:

\[ \int_{-L/2}^{+L/2} \frac{dx}{(x^2 + d^2)^{3/2}} = \int_{-L/(2d)}^{L/(2d)} \frac{d \, du}{[d^2(u^2 + 1)]^{3/2}} = \frac{1}{d^2} \int_{-L/(2d)}^{L/(2d)} \frac{du}{(u^2 + 1)^{3/2}} \]

Using the standard result \(\int \frac{du}{(u^2 + 1)^{3/2}} = \frac{u}{\sqrt{u^2 + 1}}\):

\[ \frac{1}{d^2} \left[ \frac{u}{\sqrt{u^2 + 1}} \right]_{-L/(2d)}^{L/(2d)} = \frac{1}{d^2} \left[ \frac{L/(2d)}{\sqrt{(L/(2d))^2 + 1}} - \frac{-L/(2d)}{\sqrt{(-L/(2d))^2 + 1}} \right] \]

Since \(\sqrt{(-L/(2d))^2 + 1} = \sqrt{(L/(2d))^2 + 1}\):

\[ \frac{1}{d^2} \left[ \frac{L/(2d)}{\sqrt{(L/(2d))^2 + 1}} + \frac{L/(2d)}{\sqrt{(L/(2d))^2 + 1}} \right] = \frac{1}{d^2} \cdot \frac{L/d}{\sqrt{(L/(2d))^2 + 1}} \]

Simplifying:

\[ \frac{L}{d^3} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{L^2/(4d^2) + 1}} = \frac{L}{d^3} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + d^2}/d} = \frac{L}{d^2\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + d^2}} \]

Therefore:

\[ E_y = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \lambda \, d \cdot \frac{L}{d^2\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + d^2}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{L} \cdot d \cdot \frac{L}{d^2\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + d^2}} \]

Simplifying further:

\[ E_y = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot Q \cdot \frac{1}{d\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + d^2}} \]

This is our final expression for the electric field at a point along the perpendicular bisector of the uniformly charged rod.

Part (b):
Now we evaluate the field at \(d = 0.20\,\text{m}\) using the given values: \(L = 0.50\,\text{m}\), \(Q = 1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}\), and \(\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}\).

\[ E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot Q \cdot \frac{1}{d\sqrt{(L/2)^2 + d^2}} \]

\[ E = \frac{1}{4\pi \cdot 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}} \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot \frac{1}{(0.20\,\text{m})\sqrt{(0.50/2)^2 + (0.20)^2\,\text{m}^2}} \]

\[ E = (9.0 \times 10^9\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot \frac{1}{(0.20\,\text{m})\sqrt{(0.25)^2 + (0.20)^2\,\text{m}^2}} \]

\[ E = (9.0 \times 10^9\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot \frac{1}{(0.20\,\text{m})\sqrt{0.0625 + 0.04\,\text{m}^2}} \]

\[ E = (9.0 \times 10^9\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot \frac{1}{(0.20\,\text{m})\sqrt{0.1025\,\text{m}^2}} \]

\[ E = (9.0 \times 10^9\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot \frac{1}{(0.20\,\text{m})(0.320\,\text{m})} \]

\[ E = (9.0 \times 10^9\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot \frac{1}{0.064\,\text{m}^2} \]

\[ E = (9.0 \times 10^9\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (1.0 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C}) \cdot (15.625\,\text{m}^{-2}) \]

\[ E = 1.41 \times 10^4\,\text{N/C} \]

Therefore, the electric field at a distance \(d = 0.20\,\text{m}\) from the center of the rod is \(1.41 \times 10^4\,\text{N/C}\).

Solution to Problem 9 (Electric Field on the Axis of a Charged Ring):

Part (a):
For a ring of radius \(R\) carrying total charge \(Q\), the field at a point on the axis, a distance \(z\) from the center, is: \[ E_z=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{Q\,z}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}. \]

Part (b):
With \(R=0.10\,m\), \(Q=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\), and \(z=0.15\,m\): \[ E \approx 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{(2.0\times10^{-7})\,(0.15)}{\bigl((0.10)^2+(0.15)^2\bigr)^{3/2}}, \] a value usually in the \(10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}\) range.

Solution to Problem 10 (Superposition of Electric Fields):

Given: \(q_1 = +1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \((0,0)\), and \(q_2 = -1.5\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \((0.20\,m,0)\). Compute the net field at \((0.10\,m,0)\).

Step 1: Field from \(q_1\)
Distance to the field point is \(0.10\,m\). \[ E_1=k\frac{|q_1|}{(0.10)^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{0.01}\approx8.99\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}, \] directed right (\(+x\)).

Step 2: Field from \(q_2\)
Distance to the field point is also \(0.10\,m\). \[ E_2=k\frac{|q_2|}{(0.10)^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.5\times10^{-7}}{0.01}\approx1.35\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}, \] but since \(q_2\) is negative, the field points toward \(q_2\) (i.e. also along \(+x\)).

Step 3: Net Field
\[ E_{\text{net}}=E_1+E_2\approx8.99\times10^4+1.35\times10^5=2.25\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}, \] entirely in the \(+x\) direction.
