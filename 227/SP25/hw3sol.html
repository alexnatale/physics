Homework 3 Solutions: Gauss’s Law and Electric Dipoles

Solution to Problem 1 (Spherical Gaussian Surface):

Part (a):
For a spherical surface of radius \(R=0.20\,m\) enclosing a point charge \(q=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\), Gauss’s Law states that \[ \Phi_E=\frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}. \] Since the point charge is enclosed, we have \[ \Phi_E=\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}. \]

Part (b):
Numerically, substituting \(q=1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(\varepsilon_0\approx8.85\times10^{-12}\,F/m\): \[ \Phi_E=\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{8.85\times10^{-12}} \approx1.13\times10^4\,\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 2 (Infinite Plane of Charge):

Part (a):
Consider a “pillbox” Gaussian surface that straddles an infinite plane carrying surface charge density \(\sigma\). The flux through both flat faces is \[ 2EA=\frac{\sigma A}{\varepsilon_0}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E=\frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}. \]

Part (b):
For \(\sigma=5.0\times10^{-6}\,C/m^2\): \[ E=\frac{5.0\times10^{-6}}{2(8.85\times10^{-12})}\approx2.82\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 3 (Uniformly Charged Cylinder):

A long cylinder of radius \(R=0.10\,m\) carries a uniform volume charge density \(\rho=2.0\times10^4\,C/m^3\).

Part (a):
Inside the Cylinder \((r
Choose a coaxial cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius \(r\) and length \(L\). The enclosed charge is \[ Q_{\text{enc}}=\rho\,\pi r^2L. \] The flux is given by \[ E(2\pi rL)=\frac{\rho\,\pi r^2L}{\varepsilon_0}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r)=\frac{\rho\,r}{2\varepsilon_0}. \]
Outside the Cylinder \((r\ge R)\):
The enclosed charge is the full charge of the cylinder, \(Q_{\text{enc}}=\rho\,\pi R^2L\), so \[ E(2\pi rL)=\frac{\rho\,\pi R^2L}{\varepsilon_0}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E(r)=\frac{\rho\,R^2}{2\varepsilon_0\,r}. \]

Part (b):
At \(r=0.05\,m\) (inside): \[ E(0.05)=\frac{(2.0\times10^4)(0.05)}{2(8.85\times10^{-12})}\approx5.65\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{N/C}, \] and at \(r=0.15\,m\) (outside): \[ E(0.15)=\frac{(2.0\times10^4)(0.10)^2}{2(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.15)}\approx7.53\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{N/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 4 (Uniformly Charged Sphere):

For a sphere of radius \(R\) with a uniform charge density \(\rho\), the total charge is \[ Q=\rho\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3. \]

Part (a):
Inside \((r By symmetry, a Gaussian surface inside the sphere gives \[ E(r)=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{3Q}{R^3}\,r. \]
Outside \((r\ge R)\): For \(r\ge R\), the entire charge is enclosed: \[ E(r)=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2}. \]

Part (b):
For \(R=0.15\,m\) and \(\rho=1.0\times10^4\,C/m^3\), we first compute \[ Q=\rho\frac{4}{3}\pi (0.15)^3\approx141.4\,C. \] Then, at \(r=0.10\,m\) (inside the sphere): \[ \begin{align} E_{\text{inside}}(0.10) &= \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{3Q}{R^3}(0.10)\\ &= 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{3 \times 141.4}{(0.15)^3} \times 0.10\\ &= 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{424.2}{0.003375} \times 0.10\\ &= 8.99\times10^9 \times 125,689 \times 0.10\\ &\approx 1.13\times10^{14}\,\text{N/C} \end{align} \] At \(r=0.30\,m\) (outside the sphere): \[ \begin{align} E_{\text{outside}}(0.30) &= \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{(0.30)^2}\\ &= 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{141.4}{(0.30)^2}\\ &= 8.99\times10^9 \times \frac{141.4}{0.09}\\ &= 8.99\times10^9 \times 1,571.1\\ &\approx 1.41\times10^{13}\,\text{N/C} \end{align} \] Note: These field values are extremely high due to the large charge density specified in the problem. In typical laboratory conditions, electric fields are usually many orders of magnitude smaller.

Solution to Problem 5 (Spherical Shell):

A spherical shell of radius \(R=0.25\,m\) carries a uniformly distributed total charge \(Q=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\).

Part (a):
By Gauss’s Law, the net electric flux through any closed surface enclosing the shell is \[ \Phi_E=\frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}. \]

Part (b):
Numerically, \[ \Phi_E=\frac{2.0\times10^{-7}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}\approx2.26\times10^4\,\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C}. \]

Solution to Problem 6 (Torque on an Electric Dipole):

An electric dipole consists of charges \(+q\) and \(-q\) separated by a distance \(d\), so its dipole moment is \[ p=q\,d. \] For \(q=2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(d=0.04\,m\), \[ p=2.0\times10^{-7}\times0.04=8.0\times10^{-9}\,C\cdot m. \]

When placed in a uniform external field \(\mathbf{E}_0=5.0\times10^3\,N/C\) making an angle of \(30^\circ\) with the dipole, the torque is given by \[ \tau=p\,E_0\sin\theta. \] Substituting the values and using \(\sin30^\circ=0.5\): \[ \tau=8.0\times10^{-9}\times5.0\times10^3\times0.5=2.0\times10^{-5}\,N\cdot m. \]

Solution to Problem 7 (Potential Energy of an Electric Dipole):

The potential energy \(U\) of an electric dipole in a uniform external electric field is given by \[ U=-\mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{E}_0=-pE_0\cos\theta. \] For a dipole with \(q=2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(d=0.04\,m\), \[ p=8.0\times10^{-9}\,C\cdot m. \] With \(\theta=45^\circ\) (so \(\cos45^\circ\approx0.7071\)) and \(E_0=5.0\times10^3\,N/C\): \[ U=-8.0\times10^{-9}\times5.0\times10^3\times0.7071\approx-2.83\times10^{-5}\,J. \]

Solution to Problem 8 (Electric Field on the Perpendicular Bisector of a Dipole):

The magnitude of the electric field on the perpendicular bisector of an ideal dipole is \[ E_{\text{perp}}=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{p}{r^3}, \] where the dipole moment is \(p=q\,d\). For \(q=1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(d=0.05\,m\), \[ p=1.0\times10^{-7}\times0.05=5.0\times10^{-9}\,C\cdot m. \] At a distance \(r=0.25\,m\): \[ E_{\text{perp}}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{5.0\times10^{-9}}{(0.25)^3}. \] Since \((0.25)^3=0.015625\), \[ E_{\text{perp}}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{5.0\times10^{-9}}{0.015625}\approx2.88\times10^3\,N/C. \]
Note: The field on the perpendicular bisector is directed opposite to the dipole moment.

Solution to Problem 9 (Work Done to Rotate an Electric Dipole):

The work required to rotate an electric dipole from an initial angle \(0^\circ\) to a final angle \(\theta\) in a uniform electric field is given by \[ W=p\,E_0\,(1-\cos\theta). \] For a dipole with \(q=1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(d=0.05\,m\), \[ p=1.0\times10^{-7}\times0.05=5.0\times10^{-9}\,C\cdot m. \] With \(E_0=5.0\times10^3\,N/C\) and \(\theta=60^\circ\) (where \(\cos60^\circ=0.5\)): \[ W=5.0\times10^{-9}\times5.0\times10^3\times(1-0.5)=5.0\times10^{-9}\times5.0\times10^3\times0.5. \] Evaluating, \[ W=1.25\times10^{-5}\,J. \]

Solution to Problem 10 (Superposition of Electric Fields from a Point Charge and a Dipole):

A point charge \(q=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) is at \((0,0)\) and an electric dipole is centered at \((0.20\,m,0)\) with charges \(+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(-1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) separated by \(d=0.05\,m\). We wish to find the net electric field at the point \((0.10\,m,0)\).

Step 1: Field from the Point Charge
The distance from \((0,0)\) to \((0.10,0)\) is \(0.10\,m\). Thus, \[ E_1=k\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.10)^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{0.01}\approx8.99\times10^4\,N/C. \] This field points radially away from the positive charge, i.e. along the \(+x\) direction.

Step 2: Field from the Dipole
Assume the dipole is aligned along the \(x\)-axis. With the dipole centered at \((0.20,0)\):
- The positive charge is at \((0.20+0.025,\,0)=(0.225,0)\).
- The negative charge is at \((0.20-0.025,\,0)=(0.175,0)\).

Field from the Positive Charge:
The distance from \((0.225,0)\) to \((0.10,0)\) is \[ r_{+}=0.225-0.10=0.125\,m. \] Its field magnitude is \[ E_{+}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.125)^2}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{0.015625}\approx5.75\times10^4\,N/C. \] At \((0.10,0)\), the field due to the positive charge points toward the direction away from the charge (i.e. from \((0.225,0)\) to \((0.10,0)\)), which is in the \(-x\) direction.

Field from the Negative Charge:
The distance from \((0.175,0)\) to \((0.10,0)\) is \[ r_{-}=0.175-0.10=0.075\,m. \] Its field magnitude is \[ E_{-}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.075)^2}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{0.005625}\approx1.60\times10^5\,N/C. \] For a negative charge the field points toward the charge; here, that is in the \(+x\) direction.

Step 3: Net Dipole Field
The contributions from the dipole are opposite in direction: \[ E_{\text{dipole}}=E_{-}-E_{+}\approx1.60\times10^5-5.75\times10^4\approx1.02\times10^5\,N/C, \] directed along \(+x\).

Step 4: Superposition
Adding the field from the point charge, \[ E_{\text{net}}=E_1+E_{\text{dipole}}\approx8.99\times10^4+1.02\times10^5\approx1.91\times10^5\,N/C. \]
Part (b):
The resulting net electric field at \((0.10\,m,0)\) is approximately \(1.91\times10^5\,N/C\) directed entirely along the \(+x\) axis.
