Homework 1 Solutions: Vectors, Electric Charge, and Coulomb's Law

Solution to Problem 1 (Advanced Vector Operations):

Part (a):
Given \(\mathbf{A} = (3,\,-4,\,2)\).
Compute the magnitude:
\[ |\mathbf{A}| = \sqrt{3^2+(-4)^2+2^2} = \sqrt{9+16+4} = \sqrt{29}. \]
The unit vector is:
\[ \hat{A} = \left(\frac{3}{\sqrt{29}},\,\frac{-4}{\sqrt{29}},\,\frac{2}{\sqrt{29}}\right). \]

Part (b):
For \(\mathbf{B} = (1,\,2,\,-2)\), compute the dot product:
\[ \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = 3\cdot1 + (-4)\cdot2 + 2\cdot(-2) = 3-8-4 = -9. \]
The negative sign indicates that the angle between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) is obtuse.

Part (c):
Compute the cross product \( \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} \) using the formula
\[ \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} = (a_2b_3 - a_3b_2,\; a_3b_1 - a_1b_3,\; a_1b_2 - a_2b_1). \]
Substituting \(\mathbf{A}=(3,-4,2)\) and \(\mathbf{B}=(1,2,-2)\):
- \(x\)-component: \((-4)(-2) - (2)(2)=8-4=4\),
- \(y\)-component: \((2)(1) - (3)(-2)=2+6=8\),
- \(z\)-component: \((3)(2) - (-4)(1)=6+4=10\).
Thus, \(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} = (4,\,8,\,10)\).
Verification: \( \mathbf{A}\cdot(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B})=0 \) and \( \mathbf{B}\cdot(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B})=0 \).

Solution to Problem 2 (Vector Addition & Decomposition):

Part (a):
With \(\mathbf{C}=(2,3)\) and \(\mathbf{D}=(-1,4)\), add component‐wise:
\[ \mathbf{R}=\mathbf{C}+\mathbf{D}=(2+(-1),\; 3+4)=(1,7). \]

Part (b):
Magnitude of \(\mathbf{R}\):
\[ |\mathbf{R}|=\sqrt{1^2+7^2}=\sqrt{1+49}=\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt{2}. \]
Direction (angle from the +x‑axis):
\[ \theta=\arctan\left(\frac{7}{1}\right)\approx81.87^\circ. \]

Part (c):
The magnitude computed from the components confirms the result in (b).

Solution to Problem 3 (Projection and Angle Between Vectors):

Part (a):
For \(\mathbf{A}=(4,-2,1)\) and \(\mathbf{B}=(1,1,0)\):
Calculate the dot product:
\[ \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}=4\cdot1+(-2)\cdot1+1\cdot0=4-2=2. \]
The projection of \(\mathbf{A}\) onto \(\mathbf{B}\) is:
\[ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{B}}\mathbf{A}=\left(\frac{\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}}{|\mathbf{B}|^2}\right)\mathbf{B}. \]
Since \(|\mathbf{B}|=\sqrt{1^2+1^2}=\sqrt{2}\), then \(|\mathbf{B}|^2=2\). Hence,
\[ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{B}}\mathbf{A}=\frac{2}{2}(1,1,0)=(1,1,0). \]

Part (b):
To find the angle \(\theta\) between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\), use
\[ \cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}}{|\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|}. \]
First, compute \(|\mathbf{A}|=\sqrt{4^2+(-2)^2+1^2}=\sqrt{16+4+1}=\sqrt{21}\). Then,
\[ \cos\theta=\frac{2}{\sqrt{21}\sqrt{2}}=\frac{2}{\sqrt{42}}, \]
so \(\theta=\arccos\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{42}}\right)\).

Solution to Problem 4 (Resultant of Multiple Vectors):

Given \(\mathbf{P}=(2,0,3)\), \(\mathbf{Q}=(-1,4,0)\), and \(\mathbf{R}=(0,-2,5)\), add them component‑wise:
\[ \mathbf{S}=\mathbf{P}+\mathbf{Q}+\mathbf{R}=(2-1+0,\; 0+4-2,\; 3+0+5)=(1,2,8). \]
The magnitude of \(\mathbf{S}\) is:
\[ |\mathbf{S}|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+8^2}=\sqrt{1+4+64}=\sqrt{69}. \]

Solution to Problem 5 (Cross Product and Area Calculation):

Part (a):
Given \(\mathbf{U}=(1,3,-2)\) and \(\mathbf{V}=(4,-1,2)\), compute the cross product using the determinant method:
\[ \mathbf{U}\times\mathbf{V} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 3 & -2 \\ 4 & -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix}. \]
Components:
- \(x\): \(3\cdot2 - (-2)\cdot(-1)=6-2=4\),
- \(y\): \((-2)\cdot4 - 1\cdot2=-8-2=-10\),
- \(z\): \(1\cdot(-1)-3\cdot4=-1-12=-13\).
Thus, \(\mathbf{U}\times\mathbf{V}=(4,-10,-13)\).

Part (b):
The area of the parallelogram is the magnitude of \(\mathbf{U}\times\mathbf{V}\):
\[ \text{Area}=\sqrt{4^2+(-10)^2+(-13)^2}=\sqrt{16+100+169}=\sqrt{285}. \]

Solution to Problem 6 (Coulomb Force Between Two Charges):

Given \(q_1=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\), \(q_2=-3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and separation \(r=0.15\,m\).
(a) Using Coulomb’s law:
\[ F=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}, \] with \(k\approx8.99\times10^9\,N\cdot m^2/C^2\).
Compute \(|q_1q_2|=2.0\times10^{-7}\times3.0\times10^{-7}=6.0\times10^{-14}\) and \(r^2=0.15^2=0.0225\).
Thus, \[ F\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{6.0\times10^{-14}}{0.0225}\approx0.0240\,N. \]
(b) Because the charges are opposite in sign, the force is attractive.

Solution to Problem 7 (Superposition of Coulomb Forces):

Three charges along the x‑axis: \(q_1=+3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0\), \(q_2=-1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0.20\,m\), \(q_3=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0.35\,m\).
Force on \(q_2\) due to \(q_1\):
\[ F_{12}=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{(0.20)^2}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(3.0\times10^{-7})(1.0\times10^{-7})}{0.04}\approx6.74\times10^{-3}\,N. \]
The force is attractive (toward \(q_1\), i.e. leftward).
Force on \(q_2\) due to \(q_3\):
The separation between \(q_2\) and \(q_3\) is \(0.35-0.20=0.15\,m\).
\[ F_{32}=k\frac{|q_3q_2|}{(0.15)^2}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(2.0\times10^{-7})(1.0\times10^{-7})}{0.0225}\approx8.00\times10^{-3}\,N. \]
This force is directed toward \(q_3\) (rightward).
Net force on \(q_2\):
\[ F_{net}\approx8.00\times10^{-3}\,N-(6.74\times10^{-3}\,N)=1.26\times10^{-3}\,N, \] directed to the right.

Solution to Problem 8 (Effect of Separation on Coulomb Force):

Given \(q_1=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(q_2=-2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) with an initial separation \(r=0.10\,m\).
(a) Calculate the initial force:
\[ F=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{(0.10)^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{4.0\times10^{-14}}{0.01}\approx0.0360\,N. \]
(b) If separation increases by 20%, new \(r=0.10\times1.20=0.12\,m\).
Then, \[ F_{new}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{4.0\times10^{-14}}{(0.12)^2}\approx0.0250\,N. \]
(c) The inverse square law shows that a 20% increase in distance reduces the force by roughly \(\left(\frac{0.10}{0.12}\right)^2\approx0.69\).

Solution to Problem 9 (Equilibrium Position for a Test Charge):

Two fixed charges: \(q_1=+3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0\) and \(q_2=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0.50\,m\).
A test charge \(q_0=-1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) is placed between them.
Let the test charge be at distance \(x\) from \(q_1\) and \(0.50-x\) from \(q_2\).
The forces are given by:
\[ F_1=k\frac{|q_0q_1|}{x^2}\quad\text{and}\quad F_2=k\frac{|q_0q_2|}{(0.50-x)^2}. \]
For equilibrium, set \(F_1=F_2\): \[ \frac{3.0\times10^{-7}}{x^2}=\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.50-x)^2}. \]
Rearranging: \[ 3(0.50-x)^2=x^2. \]
Taking square roots gives: \[ \sqrt{3}(0.50-x)=x. \]
Solving: \[ x=\frac{0.50\sqrt{3}}{1+\sqrt{3}}\approx\frac{0.50\times1.732}{2.732}\approx0.317\,m. \]
Thus, the test charge should be placed about \(0.317\,m\) from \(q_1\) (and \(0.183\,m\) from \(q_2\)).

Solution to Problem 10 (Coulomb Force in a Triangular Charge Configuration):

Assumptions and Setup:
Consider an equilateral triangle with side length \(L=0.20\,m\). Place the charges at:
- \(q_A=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at vertex A \((0,0)\),
- \(q_B=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at vertex B \((0.20,0)\),
- \(q_C=+3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at vertex C \((0.10,0.1732)\) (since the height of an equilateral triangle is \(0.20\sqrt{3}/2\approx0.1732\,m\)).

Step 1: Force on \(q_A\) due to \(q_B\) (\(\vec{F}_{AB}\))
Compute the magnitude using Coulomb’s law:
\[ F_{AB}=k\frac{|q_Aq_B|}{L^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(1.0\times10^{-7})(2.0\times10^{-7})}{(0.20)^2}. \]
Evaluating, we get:
\[ F_{AB}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{2.0\times10^{-14}}{0.04}\approx4.50\times10^{-3}\,N. \]
Since the force is repulsive, it acts from \(q_B\) toward \(q_A\) (i.e. in the negative x‑direction):
\[ \vec{F}_{AB}\approx4.50\times10^{-3}\,N\,(-\hat{i}). \]

Step 2: Force on \(q_A\) due to \(q_C\) (\(\vec{F}_{AC}\))
Compute the magnitude:
\[ F_{AC}=k\frac{|q_Aq_C|}{L^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(1.0\times10^{-7})(3.0\times10^{-7})}{(0.20)^2}\approx6.74\times10^{-3}\,N. \]
Determine the direction: The vector from \(q_C\) at \((0.10,0.1732)\) to \(q_A\) at \((0,0)\) is:
\[ \vec{r}_{AC}=(0-0.10,\;0-0.1732)=(-0.10,-0.1732). \]
Its magnitude is \(0.20\,m\), so the unit vector is:
\[ \hat{r}_{AC}=\left(-\frac{0.10}{0.20},\; -\frac{0.1732}{0.20}\right)=(-0.5,-0.866). \]
Therefore, the force is:
\[ \vec{F}_{AC}\approx6.74\times10^{-3}\,N\,(-0.5\hat{i}-0.866\hat{j})\approx(-3.37\times10^{-3}\,\hat{i}-5.84\times10^{-3}\,\hat{j})\,N. \]

Step 3: Net Force on \(q_A\)
Add the forces component‑wise:
\( F_x = -4.50\times10^{-3}+(-3.37\times10^{-3}) = -7.87\times10^{-3}\,N \),
\( F_y = 0+(-5.84\times10^{-3}) = -5.84\times10^{-3}\,N \).
The net force magnitude is:
\[ |\vec{F}_{net}|=\sqrt{(-7.87\times10^{-3})^2+(-5.84\times10^{-3})^2}\approx9.80\times10^{-3}\,N. \]
And its direction (angle measured from the negative x‑axis) is:
\[ \theta=\arctan\left(\frac{5.84\times10^{-3}}{7.87\times10^{-3}}\right)\approx36.5^\circ. \]

Final Answer:
The net force on \(q_A\) is approximately \(9.80\times10^{-3}\,N\), directed \(36.5^\circ\) below the negative x‑axis.
