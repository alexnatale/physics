Problem 1: Advanced Vector Operations

  1. (a) Magnitude and Unit Vector of \(\mathbf{A}\)
    • Given \(\mathbf{A} = (3\,\hat{i} - 4\,\hat{j} + 2\,\hat{k})\), the magnitude is \[ |\mathbf{A}| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{29} \approx 5.39\,. \]
    • The unit vector is \[ \hat{A} = \frac{\mathbf{A}}{|\mathbf{A}|} \approx \langle 0.557,\, -0.743,\, 0.371 \rangle\,. \]
  2. (b) Dot Product \(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}\) and Its Sign
    • Given \(\mathbf{B} = (1\,\hat{i} + 2\,\hat{j} - 2\,\hat{k})\), we have \[ \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = 3(1) + (-4)(2) + 2(-2) = -9\,. \]
    • The negative sign indicates that the angle between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) is obtuse.
  3. (c) Cross Product \(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}\) and Verification
    • The cross product is \[ \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} = \langle 4,\, 8,\, 10 \rangle\,. \]
    • Verification: \(\mathbf{A}\cdot(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}) = 0\) and \(\mathbf{B}\cdot(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}) = 0\), confirming that \(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}\) is perpendicular to both \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\).

Results Summary:

  • \( |\mathbf{A}| \approx 5.39 \)
  • \( \hat{A} \approx \langle 0.557,\, -0.743,\, 0.371 \rangle \)
  • \( \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = -9 \)
  • \( \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} = \langle 4,\, 8,\, 10 \rangle \)

Problem 2: Vector Addition & Decomposition

  1. (a) Resultant Vector \(\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{C} + \mathbf{D}\)
    • Given \(\mathbf{C} = (2\,\hat{i} + 3\,\hat{j})\) and \(\mathbf{D} = (-1\,\hat{i} + 4\,\hat{j})\), we have \[ \mathbf{R} = (2-1)\,\hat{i} + (3+4)\,\hat{j} = \langle 1,\, 7 \rangle\,. \]
  2. (b) Polar Form (Magnitude and Angle)
    • Magnitude: \[ |\mathbf{R}| = \sqrt{1^2 + 7^2} = \sqrt{50} \approx 7.07\,. \]
    • Angle from the \(+x\)-axis: \[ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{7}{1}\right) \approx 81.87^\circ\,. \]
  3. (c) Verification
    • The magnitude computed from the components confirms the result.

Results Summary:

  • \( \mathbf{R} = \langle 1,\, 7 \rangle \)
  • \( |\mathbf{R}| \approx 7.07 \)
  • \( \theta \approx 81.87^\circ \)

Problem 3: Electric Field from Two Point Charges

  1. (a) Expressions for the Electric Fields at the Midpoint
    • Charges: \(q_1 = -2.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x = 6.00\,\text{cm}\) and \(q_2 = +2.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x = 21.0\,\text{cm}\). Converting positions to meters: \(x_1=0.06\,m\) and \(x_2=0.21\,m\).
    • The midpoint is \[ x_m = \frac{0.06 + 0.21}{2} = 0.135\,m\,. \]
    • Distance from each charge to the midpoint: \[ r = 0.135 - 0.06 = 0.075\,m\,. \]
    • Field magnitude from one charge: \[ E = \frac{k|q|}{r^2} = \frac{8.99\times10^9 \times 2.00\times10^{-7}}{(0.075)^2}\,. \]
  2. (b) Net Electric Field Calculation
    • Both fields point to the left (negative \(x\)) so that \[ E_{\text{net}} = 2E \approx 6.39\times10^{5}\, \text{N/C}\,. \]
  3. (c) Effect of the Charge Signs
    • The negative charge attracts the field toward itself and the positive charge repels the field, both contributing to a leftward net field.

Results Summary:

  • \( r = 0.075\,m \)
  • \( E \approx 3.20\times10^{5}\, \text{N/C} \) (from one charge)
  • \( E_{\text{net}} \approx 6.39\times10^{5}\, \text{N/C} \) directed in the \(-\hat{i}\) direction

Problem 4: Field at the Center of a Square

  1. (a) Geometry Setup
    • A square with edge \(a=5.00\,cm\) (or \(0.05\,m\)) is centered at the origin. The four charges (in \(nC\)) are located at:
      Top Right: \(+10.00\,nC\) at \((+0.025, +0.025)\,m\)
      Top Left: \(-20.00\,nC\) at \((-0.025, +0.025)\,m\)
      Bottom Left: \(+20.00\,nC\) at \((-0.025, -0.025)\,m\)
      Bottom Right: \(-10.00\,nC\) at \((+0.025, -0.025)\,m\)
  2. (b) Net Electric Field Calculation
    • Each charge produces a field of magnitude \[ E = \frac{k|q|}{r^2}\,, \] with distance \[ r = \sqrt{(0.025)^2 + (0.025)^2} \approx 0.03536\,m\,. \]
    • After summing the vector contributions (with proper sign conventions), the net field at the center is found to be approximately \[ \mathbf{E}_{net} \approx \langle 0,\, 1.02\times10^{5} \rangle\, \text{N/C}\,. \]

Results Summary:

  • \( \mathbf{E}_{net} \approx \langle 0,\, 1.02\times10^{5} \rangle \, \text{N/C} \) (upward)

Problem 5: Electric Field Due to a Charged Arc

  1. (a) Derivation of the Expression
    • For an arc of radius \(r=2.00\,cm = 0.02\,m\) with four segments having central angles \(\theta_1\), \(\theta_2\), \(\theta_3\), \(\theta_4\) (in radians), the field from a small segment is \[ dE=\frac{k\lambda}{r}\,d\theta\,. \]
    • Summing over the segments, the net field is given by \[ \mathbf{E}_{net}=\frac{k\lambda}{r}\sum_{i=1}^{4}\theta_i(\cos\alpha_i\,\hat{i}+\sin\alpha_i\,\hat{j})\,, \] where \(\alpha_i\) is the midpoint angle for each segment.
  2. (b) Numerical Evaluation
    • Assuming the segments cover the following intervals with midpoints: \[ \theta_1=0.5236\,rad\ (\alpha_1=15^\circ),\quad \theta_2=0.8727\,rad\ (\alpha_2=55^\circ), \] \[ \theta_3=0.5236\,rad\ (\alpha_3=95^\circ),\quad \theta_4=0.3491\,rad\ (\alpha_4=120^\circ)\,. \]
    • The summed components are approximately: \[ \sum \theta_i\cos\alpha_i \approx 0.787,\quad \sum \theta_i\sin\alpha_i \approx 1.674\,. \]
    • Hence, the net magnitude is \[ |\mathbf{E}_{net}| \approx \frac{8.99\times10^9\,\lambda}{0.02}\times 1.85 \approx 8.32\times10^{11}\,\lambda \quad \text{(N/C)}\,, \] and its direction is \[ \phi \approx \arctan\!\left(\frac{1.674}{0.787}\right) \approx 64.3^\circ\,. \]

Results Summary:

  • \( |\mathbf{E}_{net}| \approx 8.32\times10^{11}\,\lambda \) N/C
  • Direction: \(64.3^\circ\) from the \(+x\)-axis

Problem 6: Electric Dipole Field

  1. (a) Derivation Along the Axial Line
    • For a dipole with charges \(+q\) and \(-q\) separated by distance \(d\), the axial field (for \(r \gg d\)) is given by \[ E_{\text{axial}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{2qd}{r^3}\,. \]
  2. (b) Numerical Evaluation at \(r=2d\)
    • With \(q=1.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(d=0.05\,m\), setting \(r=2d=0.10\,m\) gives \[ E_{\text{axial}} \approx \frac{8.99\times10^9 \times (2\times1.00\times10^{-7}\times0.05)}{(0.10)^3} \approx 8.99\times10^4\, \text{N/C}\,. \]
  3. (c) Decay with Distance
    • The dipole field falls off as \(\propto \frac{1}{r^3}\).

Results Summary:

  • \( E_{\text{axial}} \approx 8.99\times10^4 \, \text{N/C} \) at \( r = 2d \)

Problem 7: Superposition of Potentials

  1. (a) Superposition Formula
    • The net potential due to several point charges is \[ V=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\sum_{i}\frac{q_i}{r_i}\,. \]
  2. (b) Numerical Calculation
    • With \(q_1=+3.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \((0,0)\) and \(q_2=-2.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \((0.10,0)\), and evaluating at \((0.05,0)\) (where both distances are \(0.05\,m\)), we have \[ V = \frac{8.99\times10^9}{1}\left(\frac{3.00\times10^{-7}}{0.05} + \frac{-2.00\times10^{-7}}{0.05}\right) \approx 1.80\times10^4\, \text{Volts}\,. \]
  3. (c) Advantage of Superposition
    • Because potentials are scalars, they can be directly added without considering vector directions.

Results Summary:

  • \( V \approx 1.80\times10^4 \, \text{Volts}\) at \((0.05, 0)\)

Problem 8: Field from the Potential Gradient

  1. (a) Relation
    • The electric field is given by \[ \mathbf{E}=-\nabla V\,. \]
  2. (b) Calculation for \(q=+2.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(r=0.30\,m\)
    • The potential is \[ V=\frac{kq}{r} \approx \frac{8.99\times10^9\times2.00\times10^{-7}}{0.30}\approx 5993\,V\,, \] and the field is \[ E=\frac{kq}{r^2} \approx \frac{8.99\times10^9\times2.00\times10^{-7}}{(0.30)^2}\approx 1.9978\times10^4\, \text{N/C}\,. \]
  3. (c) Discussion
    • The potential falls off as \(\frac{1}{r}\) while the electric field falls off as \(\frac{1}{r^2}\).

Results Summary:

  • \( V \approx 5993\,V \)
  • \( E \approx 1.9978\times10^4\, \text{N/C} \)

Problem 9: Potential from Multiple Charges

  1. (a) Expression via Superposition
    • For two charges, the potential is \[ V=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\left(\frac{q_1}{r_1}+\frac{q_2}{r_2}\right)\,. \]
  2. (b) Calculation at \((0.10\,m,0.05\,m)\)
    • With \(q_1=+1.00\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \((0,0)\) and \(q_2=-1.50\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \((0.20,0)\), both distances are approximately \[ r_1 \approx r_2 \approx 0.1118\,m\,. \] Thus, \[ V \approx 8.99\times10^9\left(\frac{1.00\times10^{-7} - 1.50\times10^{-7}}{0.1118}\right) \approx -4023\,V\,. \]

Results Summary:

  • \( V \approx -4023\,V \) at \((0.10, 0.05)\)

Problem 10: Field from the Potential

  1. (a) Relation
    • For a one-dimensional potential \(V(x)\), \[ E(x)=-\frac{dV}{dx}\,. \]
  2. (b) Evaluation for \(V(x)=\frac{100}{x}\)
    • Differentiating, \[ \frac{dV}{dx} = -\frac{100}{x^2}\,, \] so that \[ E(x)=\frac{100}{x^2}\,. \]
    • At \(x=0.5\,m\): \[ E(0.5)=\frac{100}{(0.5)^2}=400~\text{N/C}\,. \]
    • At \(x=1.0\,m\): \[ E(1.0)=\frac{100}{1.0^2}=100~\text{N/C}\,. \]
  3. (c) Asymptotic Behavior
    • As \(x\) increases, \(E(x)\) decreases proportional to \(\frac{1}{x^2}\).

Results Summary:

  • \( E(0.5\,m)=400~\text{N/C} \)
  • \( E(1.0\,m)=100~\text{N/C} \)
