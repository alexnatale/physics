Homework 1 Solutions: Vectors, Electric Charge, and Coulomb's Law

Solution to Problem 1 (Advanced Vector Operations):

Part (a):
Given \(\mathbf{A} = 3\hat{i} - 4\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}\).
Compute the magnitude: \[ |\mathbf{A}| = \sqrt{3^2+(-4)^2+2^2} = \sqrt{9+16+4} = \sqrt{29} \approx 5.39. \] The unit vector is: \[ \hat{A} = \frac{\mathbf{A}}{|\mathbf{A}|} = \frac{3\hat{i} - 4\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}}{\sqrt{29}} \approx 0.56\hat{i} - 0.74\hat{j} + 0.37\hat{k}. \]

Part (b):
For \(\mathbf{B} = \hat{i} + 2\hat{j} - 2\hat{k}\), compute the dot product: \[ \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = 3\cdot1 + (-4)\cdot2 + 2\cdot(-2) = 3 - 8 - 4 = -9. \] The negative sign indicates that the angle between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) is obtuse.

Part (c):
Compute the cross product \( \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} \) using the formula: \[ \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} = (a_2b_3 - a_3b_2,\; a_3b_1 - a_1b_3,\; a_1b_2 - a_2b_1). \] Substituting \(\mathbf{A} = 3\hat{i} - 4\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}\) and \(\mathbf{B} = \hat{i} + 2\hat{j} - 2\hat{k}\):
- \(x\)-component: \((-4)(-2) - (2)(2)=8-4=4\),
- \(y\)-component: \((2)(1) - (3)(-2)=2+6=8\),
- \(z\)-component: \((3)(2) - (-4)(1)=6+4=10\).
Thus, \[ \mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B} = 4\hat{i} + 8\hat{j} + 10\hat{k}. \] (No further numerical estimation is needed here as the components are exact.)

Solution to Problem 2 (Vector Addition & Decomposition):

Part (a):
With \(\mathbf{C} = 2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}\) and \(\mathbf{D} = -\hat{i} + 4\hat{j}\), add component‑wise: \[ \mathbf{R}=\mathbf{C}+\mathbf{D}=(2+(-1))\hat{i}+(3+4)\hat{j}=(\hat{i}+7\hat{j}). \]

Part (b):
Magnitude of \(\mathbf{R}\): \[ |\mathbf{R}|=\sqrt{1^2+7^2}=\sqrt{1+49}=\sqrt{50} \approx 7.07. \] Direction (angle from the +x‑axis): \[ \theta=\arctan\left(\frac{7}{1}\right)\approx81.87^\circ. \]

Part (c):
The computed magnitude confirms the result in (b).

Solution to Problem 3 (Dot Product and Angle Between Vectors):

Given \(\mathbf{A} = 4\hat{i} - 2\hat{j} + \hat{k}\) and \(\mathbf{B} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}\) (with no \(\hat{k}\) component), first compute the dot product: \[ \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}=4\cdot1 + (-2)\cdot1 + 1\cdot0 = 4 - 2 = 2. \]

Then, to find the angle \(\theta\) between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\), we use: \[ \cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}}{|\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|}. \] First, compute: \[ |\mathbf{A}|=\sqrt{4^2+(-2)^2+1^2}=\sqrt{16+4+1}=\sqrt{21} \approx 4.58, \] and \[ |\mathbf{B}|=\sqrt{1^2+1^2}=\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41. \] Thus, \[ \cos\theta=\frac{2}{4.58 \times 1.41} \approx \frac{2}{6.46} \approx 0.31, \] so \[ \theta=\arccos(0.31)\approx72.0^\circ. \]

Solution to Problem 4 (Resultant of Multiple Vectors):

Given \(\mathbf{P} = 2\hat{i} + 3\hat{k}\) (i.e. \(2\hat{i}+0\hat{j}+3\hat{k}\)), \(\mathbf{Q} = -\hat{i} + 4\hat{j}\) (i.e. \(-\hat{i}+4\hat{j}+0\hat{k}\)), and \(\mathbf{R} = -2\hat{j} + 5\hat{k}\) (i.e. \(0\hat{i}-2\hat{j}+5\hat{k}\)), add them component‑wise: \[ \mathbf{S}=\mathbf{P}+\mathbf{Q}+\mathbf{R}=(2-1+0)\hat{i}+(0+4-2)\hat{j}+(3+0+5)\hat{k}=(\hat{i}+2\hat{j}+8\hat{k}). \] The magnitude of \(\mathbf{S}\) is: \[ |\mathbf{S}|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+8^2}=\sqrt{1+4+64}=\sqrt{69} \approx 8.31. \]

Solution to Problem 5 (Cross Product and Area Calculation):

Part (a):
Given \(\mathbf{U} = \hat{i} + 3\hat{j} - 2\hat{k}\) and \(\mathbf{V} = 4\hat{i} - \hat{j} + 2\hat{k}\), compute the cross product using the determinant method: \[ \mathbf{U}\times\mathbf{V} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 3 & -2 \\ 4 & -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix}. \] Components:
- \(x\)-component: \(3\cdot2 - (-2)\cdot(-1)=6-2=4\),
- \(y\)-component: \((-2)\cdot4 - 1\cdot2=-8-2=-10\),
- \(z\)-component: \(1\cdot(-1)-3\cdot4=-1-12=-13\).
Thus, \[ \mathbf{U}\times\mathbf{V} = 4\hat{i} - 10\hat{j} - 13\hat{k}. \]

Part (b):
The area of the parallelogram defined by \(\mathbf{U}\) and \(\mathbf{V}\) is the magnitude of \(\mathbf{U}\times\mathbf{V}\): \[ \text{Area}=\sqrt{4^2+(-10)^2+(-13)^2}=\sqrt{16+100+169}=\sqrt{285} \approx 16.88. \]

Solution to Problem 6 (Coulomb Force Between Two Charges):

Given \(q_1=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(q_2=-3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) with a separation \(r=0.15\,m\).
(a) Using Coulomb’s law: \[ F=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}, \] with \(k\approx8.99\times10^9\,N\cdot m^2/C^2\).
Compute: \[ |q_1q_2| = 2.0\times10^{-7}\times3.0\times10^{-7} = 6.0\times10^{-14}\,C^2,\quad r^2 = 0.15^2 = 0.0225\,m^2. \] Thus, \[ F\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{6.0\times10^{-14}}{0.0225}\approx0.0240\,N. \] (b) Since the charges are opposite in sign, the force is attractive.

Solution to Problem 7 (Superposition of Coulomb Forces):

Three charges along the x‑axis: \(q_1=+3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0\), \(q_2=-1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0.20\,m\), and \(q_3=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0.35\,m\).
Force on \(q_2\) due to \(q_1\):
\[ F_{12}=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{(0.20)^2}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(3.0\times10^{-7})(1.0\times10^{-7})}{0.04}\approx6.74\times10^{-3}\,N. \] (Since the force is attractive, it acts toward \(q_1\), i.e. in the negative x‑direction.)
Force on \(q_2\) due to \(q_3\):
The separation between \(q_2\) and \(q_3\) is \(0.35-0.20=0.15\,m\).
\[ F_{32}=k\frac{|q_3q_2|}{(0.15)^2}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(2.0\times10^{-7})(1.0\times10^{-7})}{0.0225}\approx8.00\times10^{-3}\,N. \] (This force is directed toward \(q_3\), i.e. in the positive x‑direction.)
Net force on \(q_2\):
\[ F_{net}\approx8.00\times10^{-3}\,N - 6.74\times10^{-3}\,N = 1.26\times10^{-3}\,N, \] directed to the right.

Solution to Problem 8 (Effect of Separation on Coulomb Force):

Given \(q_1=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) and \(q_2=-2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) with an initial separation \(r=0.10\,m\).
(a) Calculate the initial force: \[ F=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{(0.10)^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{4.0\times10^{-14}}{0.01}\approx0.0360\,N. \] (b) If the separation increases by 20%, then \(r=0.10\times1.20=0.12\,m\).
\[ F_{new}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{4.0\times10^{-14}}{(0.12)^2}\approx0.0250\,N. \] (c) The inverse square law shows that increasing \(r\) reduces the force approximately by a factor of \(\left(\frac{0.10}{0.12}\right)^2\approx0.69\).

Solution to Problem 9 (Equilibrium Position for a Test Charge):

Two fixed charges: \(q_1=+3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0\) and \(q_2=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at \(x=0.50\,m\).
A test charge \(q_0=-1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) is placed between them. Let the test charge be at a distance \(x\) from \(q_1\) (the origin) and \(0.50-x\) from \(q_2\).
The forces on \(q_0\) are: \[ F_1=k\frac{|q_0q_1|}{x^2}\quad \text{and} \quad F_2=k\frac{|q_0q_2|}{(0.50-x)^2}. \] For equilibrium, set \(F_1=F_2\): \[ \frac{3.0\times10^{-7}}{x^2}=\frac{1.0\times10^{-7}}{(0.50-x)^2}. \] Rearranging: \[ 3(0.50-x)^2=x^2. \] Taking square roots: \[ \sqrt{3}(0.50-x)=x. \] Solving gives: \[ x=\frac{0.50\sqrt{3}}{1+\sqrt{3}}\approx\frac{0.50\times1.732}{2.732}\approx0.317\,m. \] Thus, the test charge should be placed about \(0.317\,m\) from \(q_1\) (and \(0.50-0.317\approx0.183\,m\) from \(q_2\)).

Solution to Problem 10 (Coulomb Force in a Triangular Charge Configuration):

Assumptions and Setup:
Consider an equilateral triangle with side length \(L=0.20\,m\). Place the charges at:
- \(q_A=+1.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at vertex A \((0,0)\),
- \(q_B=+2.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at vertex B \((0.20,0)\),
- \(q_C=+3.0\times10^{-7}\,C\) at vertex C \((0.10,0.1732)\) (the height of the triangle is \(0.20\sqrt{3}/2\approx0.1732\,m\)).

Step 1: Force on \(q_A\) due to \(q_B\) (\(\vec{F}_{AB}\))
Using Coulomb’s law: \[ F_{AB}=k\frac{|q_Aq_B|}{L^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(1.0\times10^{-7})(2.0\times10^{-7})}{(0.20)^2}. \] Evaluating: \[ F_{AB}\approx8.99\times10^9\times\frac{2.0\times10^{-14}}{0.04}\approx4.50\times10^{-3}\,N. \] The force is repulsive, acting from \(q_B\) to \(q_A\) (i.e. in the negative \(\hat{i}\) direction): \[ \vec{F}_{AB}\approx4.50\times10^{-3}\,N\,(-\hat{i}). \]

Step 2: Force on \(q_A\) due to \(q_C\) (\(\vec{F}_{AC}\))
Compute the magnitude: \[ F_{AC}=k\frac{|q_Aq_C|}{L^2}=8.99\times10^9\times\frac{(1.0\times10^{-7})(3.0\times10^{-7})}{(0.20)^2}\approx6.74\times10^{-3}\,N. \] Determine the direction: The vector from \(q_C\) at \((0.10,0.1732)\) to \(q_A\) at \((0,0)\) is: \[ \vec{r}_{AC} = -0.10\,\hat{i} - 0.1732\,\hat{j}. \] Its magnitude is \(0.20\,m\), so the unit vector is: \[ \hat{r}_{AC} = -0.5\,\hat{i} - 0.866\,\hat{j}. \] Thus, \[ \vec{F}_{AC}\approx6.74\times10^{-3}\,N\,(-0.5\,\hat{i}-0.866\,\hat{j}) \approx -3.37\times10^{-3}\,\hat{i} - 5.84\times10^{-3}\,\hat{j}\,N. \]

Step 3: Net Force on \(q_A\)
Sum the forces component‑wise:
\( F_x = -4.50\times10^{-3} + (-3.37\times10^{-3}) = -7.87\times10^{-3}\,N \),
\( F_y = 0 + (-5.84\times10^{-3}) = -5.84\times10^{-3}\,N \).
The net force magnitude is: \[ |\vec{F}_{net}| = \sqrt{(-7.87\times10^{-3})^2+(-5.84\times10^{-3})^2} \approx 9.80\times10^{-3}\,N, \] and the direction (angle measured from the negative \(\hat{i}\) direction) is: \[ \theta = \arctan\left(\frac{5.84\times10^{-3}}{7.87\times10^{-3}}\right) \approx 36.5^\circ. \]

Final Answer:
The net force on \(q_A\) is approximately \(9.80\times10^{-3}\,N\), directed \(36.5^\circ\) below the negative \(\hat{i}\) (x‑axis).
