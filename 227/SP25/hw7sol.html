Problem 1 Solution (Lorentz‑Force Concepts & Calculation)

  1. The magnetic force on a moving charge is \( \mathbf{F} = q\,\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} \). For a positive charge, the right‑hand rule (fingers from \( \mathbf{v} \) to \( \mathbf{B} \), thumb gives \( \mathbf{F} \)) indicates the direction; for a negative charge the force points opposite that direction.
  2. With \( \mathbf{v}=5.00\times10^{3}\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\,\text{m/s} \) and \( \mathbf{B}=0.50\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}\,\text{T} \), \( \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B} = -2.50\times10^{3}\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\,\text{m·T/s} \). Multiplying by \( q = 1.00\times10^{-7}\,\text{C} \) gives \( \mathbf{F} = -2.5\times10^{-4}\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\;\text{N} \).
  3. If \( \mathbf{v} \) is parallel to \( \mathbf{B} \), the angle is 0°, so \( \sin\theta = 0 \) and the magnetic force is zero.
  4. Because \( |\mathbf{F}| \propto |q| \), doubling the charge doubles the magnitude of the force.

Problem 2 Solution (Uniform Circular Motion of a Charged Particle)

  1. \( r = \dfrac{m v}{|q| B} = \dfrac{(2.00\times10^{-3})\,(3.00\times10^{3})}{(1.00\times10^{-7})(0.75)} = 8.0\times10^{7}\,\text{m} \).
  2. Cyclotron angular frequency: \( \omega = \dfrac{|q|B}{m} = 3.75\times10^{-5}\,\text{rad/s} \). Period: \( T = \dfrac{2\pi}{\omega} \approx 1.68\times10^{5}\,\text{s} \;(46.6\,\text{h}) \).
  3. With mass halved, \( r \propto m \) is halved, while \( \omega \propto 1/m \) doubles, so the frequency doubles.

Problem 3 Solution (Field Around a Long Straight Wire)

  1. \( B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(5.00)}{2\pi(0.10)} = 1.0\times10^{-5}\,\text{T} \;(10\,\mu\text{T}) \).
  2. Field lines form concentric circles centered on the wire. Point your right‑hand thumb along the current; your curled fingers show the field direction.
  3. Since \( B \propto I \), doubling the current doubles the field.

Problem 4 Solution (Field at the Center of a Circular Loop)

  1. Single loop: \( B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2r} = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(3.00)}{2(0.15)} = 1.26\times10^{-5}\,\text{T} \;(12.6\,\mu\text{T}) \).
  2. For \( N = 25 \) turns: \( B = N B_{\text{single}} = 25(1.26\times10^{-5}) = 3.14\times10^{-4}\,\text{T} \;(314\,\mu\text{T}) \).
  3. Each turn contributes the same field at the center, so the total adds linearly with \( N \). Inter‑turn interactions are negligible, hence the field scales linearly, not quadratically.

Problem 5 Solution (Field Inside a Long Solenoid)

  1. \( B = \mu_0 n I = (4\pi\times10^{-7})(500)(2.00) = 1.26\times10^{-3}\,\text{T} \;(1.26\,\text{mT}) \).
  2. Energy density: \( u = \dfrac{B^{2}}{2\mu_0} = 0.628\,\text{J·m}^{-3} \).
  3. Near the ends the field lines spread outward (fringe), so \( B \) decreases; the ideal formula assumes an infinitely long solenoid where edge effects vanish.

Problem 6 Solution (Field Inside a Toroid)

  1. Mean radius \( r = (a+b)/2 = 0.07\,\text{m} \). \( B = \dfrac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r} = 3.43\times10^{-3}\,\text{T} \;(3.43\,\text{mT}) \).
  2. Approximate core volume \( V \approx 1.38\times10^{-4}\,\text{m}^{3} \). Energy density \( u = B^{2}/(2\mu_0) = 4.68\,\text{J·m}^{-3} \). Total energy \( U = uV \approx 6.5\times10^{-4}\,\text{J} \).
  3. Because \( B \propto 1/r \), increasing the inner radius \( a \) (keeping \( b \) fixed) increases the mean radius, thus reducing the field at that radius.

Problem 7 Solution (Magnetic Dipole Moment & Energy)

  1. \( \mu = I\pi r^{2} = (4.00)(\pi)(0.10)^{2} = 1.26\times10^{-1}\,\text{A·m}^{2} \).
  2. Maximum potential energy (\( \boldsymbol{\mu} \perp \mathbf{B} \)): \( U_{\max} = \mu B = (1.26\times10^{-1})(0.30) = 3.77\times10^{-2}\,\text{J} \).
  3. Energy is minimum when \( \boldsymbol{\mu} \) aligns with \( \mathbf{B} \) because the system is then in its most stable (lowest‑energy) orientation and experiences no torque.

Problem 8 Solution (Force Between Parallel Wires)

  1. \( \dfrac{F}{L} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I^{2}}{2\pi r} = \dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(3.00)^{2}}{2\pi(0.05)} = 3.6\times10^{-5}\,\text{N·m}^{-1} \).
  2. With currents opposite, the force is repulsive; the magnetic fields exert forces pushing the wires apart.
  3. This interaction underlies the SI definition of the ampere: two straight, parallel conductors 1 m apart carrying identical currents of 1 A attract with a force of exactly \(2\times10^{-7}\,\text{N·m}^{-1}\).

Problem 9 Solution (Current Loop in a Non‑Uniform Field)

  1. The vertical sides experience unequal fields. Net force: \( F_{\text{net}} = I L\,[B(0.10) - B(0)] = (2)(0.10)[0.25(1 + 1.5) - 0.25] = 7.5\times10^{-2}\,\text{N} \) toward negative x.
  2. Torque about the loop’s center (z‑axis): \( \tau = F_{\text{net}} (L/2) \approx 3.8\times10^{-3}\,\text{N·m} \), rotating the loop so its right side moves toward the lower‑field region.
  3. Magnetic‑field gradients are exploited, for example, in MRI gradient coils and in magnetic trapping of neutral atoms.

Problem 10 Solution (Designing a Hall‑Effect Magnetic Sensor)

  1. Key design parameters: (1) carrier density \( n \), (2) device thickness \( t \) (and geometry), (3) bias current \( I \) (also mobility and temperature management).
  2. Hall voltage: \( V_H = \dfrac{I B}{n q t} = \dfrac{(0.020)(0.10)}{(8.0\times10^{22})(1.602\times10^{-19})(50\,\mu\text{m})} \approx 3.1\,\text{mV} \).
  3. Doubling the current doubles \( V_H \) but increases power \( P \propto I^{2} \) four‑fold, raising Joule heating and thermal drift.
  4. Greater sensitivity via higher current or thinner devices must be balanced against increased thermal noise and power dissipation.
